## Design Context

### Users

Three overlapping user types, each with different needs:

- **HR admins and ops managers**: configure Xero connections, manage feeds, review sync status. High data density is acceptable; they need control and confidence.
- **Team managers**: check who is out or working remotely across their team. Scanability and calendar views are primary. They visit frequently, often briefly.
- **Employees**: self-service visibility into their own availability. Surfaces they see should be lighter, less dense, more approachable.

The interface must serve all three without making any group feel like a second-class citizen.

### Brand Personality

**Modern. Calm. Precise.**

LeaveSync is a tool people trust with real business data. It should feel like a well-made instrument: composed, reliable, purposeful. It does not try to entertain. It does not overwhelm. Every screen should lower cognitive load, not raise it.

The emotional goal is **quiet confidence**: the user arrives, sees what they need, acts, and leaves. No friction, no noise.

### Aesthetic Direction

- **Primary brand colour**: `#336A3B`, a deep forest green. This is the action colour, the status signal, the identity anchor. It is used purposefully for interactive elements, confirmation states, and brand moments, not as decoration.
- **Tone**: clean, spacious, light-first. Professional without being cold.
- **Typography**: Plus Jakarta Sans (Google Fonts), a humanist geometric sans with editorial warmth. Lean into its refined precision.
- **Border radius**: `16px` (1rem) for cards and containers; `12px` for inputs and small elements. No 4px or 8px radii. Subtly rounded, not bubbly.
- **Motion**: purposeful and fast. Accordion and transition animations should feel snappy. No decorative motion.
- **Mode**: light-first. Dark mode is user-preference and should receive equal care.
- **Depth**: tonal layering (surface colour shifts) is the primary mechanism for hierarchy on persistent surfaces. Reserve translucency, backdrop blur, and soft shadows for elevated transient surfaces only: modals, popovers, dropdowns, sticky app chrome, toasts, mobile sheets. Borders remain a last resort. See DESIGN.md for surface token hierarchy.
- **Anti-reference (Notion)**: avoid the flat document aesthetic, undifferentiated text-heavy layout, lack of visual hierarchy, and low-contrast UI chrome.

### Design Principles

1. **Clarity over cleverness**: calendar and availability data must be immediately scannable. Visual hierarchy is non-negotiable. When in doubt, simplify.

2. **Green as signal, not wallpaper**: `#336A3B` earns its place on screen. Use it for primary actions, success states, and brand anchors. Resist applying it broadly as background colour or decoration.

3. **Density is role-appropriate**: admin and manager views can be denser. Employee-facing surfaces should breathe. Never sacrifice readability for compactness.

4. **Calm confidence through space**: whitespace is load-bearing. Crowded layouts erode trust. Generous padding and clear separation between sections are defaults, not luxuries.

5. **Frosted touch, used sparingly**: translucent, backdrop-blurred surfaces with soft diffused shadows are reserved for elevated transient UI (overlays, popovers, sticky chrome, toasts, sheets). They must never sit beneath primary content, dense data, calendars, or form inputs. Every frosted surface must use a tinted fill that preserves WCAG 2.2 AA contrast against worst-case content beneath, ship an opaque fallback for unsupported browsers, and honour `prefers-reduced-transparency`. The look should communicate calm modernity, not decoration.

6. **WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor**: all text, interactive elements, and status indicators must meet contrast requirements. Colour is never the sole differentiator for status.

### Language

- Australian English throughout (organise, analyse, colour, centre, prioritise).
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses.
- Direct, professional tone. No hype, cliches, or motivational language.
